Management Model — What Stayful Can Offer
📋 On the master lease question
You asked about a master lease — a fixed monthly income in exchange for Stayful taking full building access. Stayful doesn't operate on that model. Instead, Stayful manages on your behalf: marketing, booking management, guest operations, cleaning and reporting. You receive variable monthly income based on actual performance, minus Stayful's management fee. As discussed, this means the income fluctuates with the market rather than being fixed — but the average and upside is significantly higher than a fixed arrangement would typically offer.

Phased Rollout Strategy — Recommended Approach
The concern: Putting 20 short-let units onto a local market simultaneously could oversupply demand and suppress rates. The phased model avoids this while maximising returns.
Phase 1 — Start
Top floor only — STL
4–6 units on short-let. Remaining floors on long-let for income stability. Monitor occupancy and rates closely over first 6–12 months.
Phase 2 — Expand
Next floor — if demand holds
If short-let occupancy remains strong and nightly rates are not being compressed, convert the next floor to STL. Repeat floor by floor.
Phase 3 — Lock In
Optimal mix identified
If adding another floor causes demand to soften or rates to drop, hold at current level. You've found the optimal STL/LTL blend for this building.

Your Questions — Answered Honestly
How accurate is the Stayful analyser for a new area like Bridlington?+

The analyser is built on actual Airbnb booking data from comparable properties in the same area — not hypothetical estimates. It looks at real performance data from existing listings within the postcode area. The caveat is always that it's projecting from comparable properties rather than the actual unit, so real performance can be higher or lower. In Bridlington's case, Zac flagged that competition in the analyser was notably low — which typically means the projections are on the conservative side rather than optimistic. Low supply relative to demand tends to support stronger actual returns.

You can't do a master lease — so how does variable income affect my business model?+

For a development project like this, the key difference is that instead of a guaranteed fixed rent (£X/month regardless of occupancy), your income fluctuates with bookings. In strong months — July and August — you'd see over £2,000 net per unit. In quiet months — November — closer to £770. The annual total of around £16,800 net per unit compares favourably to long-let net income of approximately £11,880 per year after agent fees. The phased approach helps here too: keeping some floors on long-let gives you a predictable baseline while the short-let floors capture the upside.

What happens if 20 short-let units in the same area compete with each other?+

That's exactly the right question to ask — and the reason the phased approach matters. Starting with one floor (typically 4–6 units) allows you to test local STL demand before committing more capacity. If those units perform well and occupancy stays strong, you expand to the next floor. If you notice demand softening — occupancy dropping, rates needing to come down to fill rooms — you hold at that level. The Bridlington market currently shows low STL competition, which suggests there's headroom to absorb capacity. But the phased model means you never overshoot.

Does Stayful cover areas outside Bridlington for my other projects?+

Yes — Stayful operates nationwide. The same management model applies regardless of location: listings, dynamic pricing, guest management, cleaning coordination and monthly reporting are all handled centrally, with local cleaning and maintenance teams in each area on an approved framework. If you have projects in other areas — particularly those closer to completion than this one — Stayful can run a separate income analysis and discuss terms. The conversation doesn't have to wait for this particular project to progress.

What's the honest worst case if short-let returns in Bridlington underperform?+

The worst case is that you pull back the short-let operation and move affected units to long-let — at around £1,485 gross per month (£990 net after agent fees). That's the floor. The phased approach means you'd never have the whole building committed to short-let before you've verified demand, so the downside is always contained. Year one returns are projected at approximately £7,865 per unit — lower than the Year 2 run rate because the listing needs time to build reviews and occupancy. If the market proves weaker than projected, you'd lock in that point and convert the remaining units to long-let.
