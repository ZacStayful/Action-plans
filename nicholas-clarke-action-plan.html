Your Questions — Answered Honestly
The initial figures you showed were £73k — how confident are you in the £34k–£43k range?+

Completely fair question, and I want to be straight with you. The initial software figures were generated using your property as a one-bedroom flat — not a studio. That's a meaningful difference because studios compete more with hotels than with standard Airbnb properties, and the algorithm doesn't handle that well. The revised range of £34k–£43k gross is based on two actual Oxford properties I identified during our call: a nearby large double room achieving £34k annually, and a self-contained annex achieving £43k. Your property sits somewhere between these — self-contained and well-fitted, which is genuinely better than a shared double room, but without the parking of the annex. Oxford also has some of the highest nightly rates in the country for short lets, which supports this range. The corrected figures are the ones I'm confident in.

My mortgage has a 90-day cap on short lets. How does this affect income?+

The 90-day cap applies to stays under 28 nights. So your plan of summer short-lets and winter long-lets is a sensible starting point. There's also an opportunity worth exploring: bookings of 28 nights or longer typically don't count toward the 90-day restriction. If your mortgage lender confirms this (and I'd strongly encourage you to check), we could run a blended approach — short-stay bookings on weekends or shorter breaks alongside a longer-term occupant during quieter months — potentially without ever triggering the cap. Even under the restricted approach, the combined summer STL plus winter long-let income should be competitive with a full-year long let.

What compliance do I actually need for a studio STL?+

There's no official statutory registration scheme for short lets in Oxford at the time of writing — so no mandatory licensing. What you do need: a valid EPC (yours is recent, so you're covered for ~5 years), annual gas safety certificate if you have gas appliances, and valid buildings and contents insurance that permits short-term letting. PAT testing is not a legal requirement but is good practice and inexpensive. We'll flag anything relevant when we do the initial inspection.

Zac mentioned a trading company structure for tax — is that really worth it?+

I'm not an accountant so I can't give formal advice, but the principle I outlined is real: if you set up a company that holds the short-let operation, and that company pays you a rent (as a lodger allowance) of up to around £7,500 per year, you can extract that income tax-free. Any profit above that is subject to corporation tax (currently 19–25%), which may be lower than your personal income tax rate. The trading company structure is commonly used by property investors. Worth discussing with a tax adviser who specialises in property — they should give you a quick yes/no on whether this makes sense for your situation.

What's the honest worst case if the income projections aren't achieved?+

Year One will be lower than Year Two — that's built into the projections, and I've shown you Year One at 80% of the full run rate. The honest floor is: if bookings are slow in the first one to two months (which happens occasionally with new listings), you're still protected by the 90-day flexibility — you can fill any gaps with a long let without significant cost. Oxford is a genuinely strong market and the ramp-up period is typically short. But I won't pretend there's zero risk. If you want to proceed conservatively, the long-let at £17,366 a year is your floor — and any STL performance above that is upside.
